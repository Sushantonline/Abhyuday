Fin Flutter Analysis

Fins have flutter velocity, above which they have a tendency to fail resulting in unstable flight. For the fins to not flutter, the dimensions of the fins should be optimised such that the Maximum Safe Speed is greater than the Maximum speed at which the rocket is operated.

Nose Cone Design

For the rocket, a Von-Karman shaped nose cone has been selected with a fineness ratio of 4. Based on the requirements of payloads, the length of shoulder was constrained to 35cm & inner diameter as 14.8cm. The payload will be placed inside the shoulder of Nose cone & the shoulder will be covered with another body tube.

Simulations were run in the nose cone at Mach 0.85 to check the wall temperature. The analysis indicated that the temperature on the surface isn’t too high to affect the material.

However, the tip may not experience significant temperature but it surely experiences strong pressure. Thus a metal tip has been placed in front of the nose cone which will handle the high pressure & also simplify the manufacturing process of the entire nose cone. The material of choice for tip is Aluminium.

The material for the remaining portion of the nose cone is selected as carbon fibre.

Transition

The transition portion will be the connecting component between upper and lower body tubes. The shoulder added on the front & aft of the transition will be connected to the body tubes. The material of choice for this component is Aluminium considering the complexity of shape. The conical part has a thickness of 4mm, whereas both the shoulders are 2mm thick & 5cm long.

Canard System

Sizing of Canards

The sizing of canards are determined to ensure the stabilising effect of roll is not discounted while ensuring there is least energy loss during the launch. In order to control the rocket’s roll, the canards must be designed to provide sufficient lift and generate a roll moment that can counter disturbances. To determine the size of the canards required, simulations were conducted on the current design of the rocket with maximum fin cant of 3 degrees. A maximum roll rate of 3.65 rps was obtained which is significantly higher to the desirable case. This roll rate corresponded to roll moment coefficient range of 0.18 to 0.67, which was determined through open rocket software. A benchmark of 0.6 was set to determine the dimensions of the canard. This value is expected to overcome roll induced due to manufacturing asymmetry while avoiding maximum control of roll. The following formula was utilised to determine the total planform area of the canards system

C_l = 2*pi*Delta_max*(S_canard*R_canard)/S_ref*L_ref)

Where

C_l: Roll moment coefficient

Delta_max: Maximum canard deflection angle

S_canard: Total planform area of canards

R_canard: Radius to centre of pressure of canards

S_ref: Reference area of rocket
L_ref: Reference length of rocket

Our team decided to go with a 4 canard system to create more control on the roll parameters and maintain symmetry. Through the formula, for a maximum canard deflection of 15 degrees,  we obtained the surface area of each canard to approximately be 10.5 sq cms. A safety factor of around 3.5 was considered to obtain a working planform area of canard to be 37.5 sq cms.

The canards have a trapezoidal shape and are symmetrically designed with the following dimensions, root chord - 6cm, tip chord- 4cm and height - 7.5 cm.

Further simulations indicated the canards provide a maximum roll coefficient of 0.72 which exceeds the goal roll coefficient of 0.6 required to overcome expected rocket roll due to rocket asymmetry. Therefore, the canard design has exceeding control authority.
To reduce the complexity involved with manufacturing, the Edge tapered model was used throughout.
Cavity is to facilitate the attachment of the axle to the canard.

Mechanical Design[a]

The mechanical design of the canard system has been done with the aim to have quick & easy assembly of the system. Whole system will be stacked inside a dedicated body tube, between 2 bulkheads. The lower bulkhead will be rigidly fixed with the lower bulkhead. The 4 Servo motors[b] will be mounted on a 3D printed arrangement.

Based on calculations of force that is expected to act on each canard, we found the expected resisting torque. The servo that has been selected is a Dynamixel XL Series servo.

In order to minimise the value of resisting torque, it was decided to keep the shaft close from the CP position. It was found using XFLR5 that CP varies within the range of 20% to 44% of mean aerodynamic chord with quarter chord point, and considering the 2.5mm diameter of shaft too, the shaft is made to pass through the quarter chord point. All the electronic components will be placed delicately on this arrangement & connected with each other.

This arrangement will then be placed & tightened on the lower bulkhead inside the body tube. The battery has been attached to the bottom side of the lower bulkhead. The wires are connected with each other & then the whole assembly will be sealed from top with another bulkhead.

The shafts of canards will be experiencing varying force in radial direction. Thus to allow smooth rotation, radial bearings have been used which will not only take the radial forces, but also allow for smooth rotation. The canard body tube has radial bearings, of inner diameter as 3mm, placed through which the shafts will pass and connected with the servo. With this, the whole arrangement will be ready to be mounted on the rocket.

Canard Electronics

The main focus behind the canard system was not to bring the roll of the rocket to zero, but to reduce it to a fixed quantity as rotation also stabilises the rocket. To achieve this we required four servos, a microcontroller, a battery and a power distribution board.

Initially we decided to go with BeagleBone Black Wireless for the idea was to wirelessly connect the canard system with the ESP32 in the avionics bay to enable angular velocity data transfer. And to power the microcontroller using the 11.1V Lipo battery, we added a LM7805 voltage regulator. We chose Dynamixel XL Series servos for the motors. This idea was later discarded as the Integration of the above components were tedious. Instead we decided to use an OpenRB microcontroller which is specifically designed for the Dynamixel Servos and also eliminated the need for a voltage regulator as it could be directly powered by 11.1 V battery source.

Flow Simulation

Rocket Body

The figures depict flow properties over the surface of the rocket.

Simulations are done considering extreme conditions i.e., maximum freestream velocity & density.

Here, there are typically 3 vital regions to be considered, from an aerodynamics point of view.

i.        The nose tip; it encounters the stagnation point, resembles higher pressure & temperature comparatively.

ii.        The rapid transition; this is a critical region because of the adverse pressure gradients and has a higher possibility that the flow will separate thus creating a wake region.

This wake region will in turn give rise to the pressure drag.

iii.        On the fins’ leading edge, it is evident that on fin’s leading there is higher pressure, considering thickness this has been taken into consideration such that it will not disturb the flow pattern.

The maximum drag for the whole configuration is calculated to be 400 newtons, however lift is negligible compared to the overall force system.

Canards

1} 90 Degrees AOA:

The picture represents the pressure variation on the control surface when oriented perpendicular to the flow field.

Here, the canard behaves as the blunt body. The flow past it creates a recirculating region or wake.

This will induce a higher pressure drag on the rocket body, which will help in achieving desired apogee.The maximum amount of pressure drag generated with this configuration is noted to be 130 Newtons.

This value is for a single canard; therefore, the whole control surface system will produce drag of as high as 520 Newtons.

This force component will lead to the pitching as well as yawing moments, it could lead the rocket to topple.

Nonetheless, 4 canards configuration are designed in a way that the moments induced by individual canard will be counter reacted with that of other canard at the centre of gravity of the vehicle.

2} 15 Degrees AOA:

Here, another objective for the canards is to control the stability of the rocket in the roll axis.

The deflected canard is having the higher pressure on the bottom surface and lower pressure on the upper surface.

Therefore, two components of aerodynamic forces (lift and drag) are generated.

Lift force will contribute towards overcoming the induced roll rate and drag will control the altitude. The roll moment generated comes from the contribution of the all four canards deflected at particular angle of attack (AOA), which will oppose the free spinning of the rocket at large rates.

The maximum values for aerodynamic forces are drag=35 Newtons and lift=82 Newtons, for single canard. Total forces (4 canards combination) value would be approximately 400 and 140 Newtons, lift and drag, respectively.

Rocket Designs:

Initially, two designs were considered on the basis of the type of transition. That is 1-Rapid transition and 2-Long transition.

·       

1}Rapid Transition: as the name suggests there is rapid transition of diameter from initially a larger diameter of nose cone to the comparatively smaller diameter of body tubes.

·        As we’re aware of the magnitude of drag faced in a rocket, this transition technique is implemented to overcome skin friction drag to an extent.

·   

This at first, might not look like a good idea in the perspective of an aerodynamic design.

·        There is a possibility of recirculating region development downstream of the transition and the initial idea of decreasing drag might not seem to be fulfilled.

·        Nevertheless, the dimensions are kept considering the effect of the wake generation are minimised. This was validated using some simulations.  

·        Furthermore, this design has a good manufacturability, strength and a simple design, all this could save a significant amount of time.

·       

2} Long transition: the transition is a streamlined body with a smooth decrement in the cross section area.

·        Here, flow separation does not happen as there is an adverse pressure gradient on the transition surface.

·        This seems to be fulfilling our objective, though the manufacturability is not appreciable and the designing is time consuming.


 Simulations were carried out for the mentioned designs and surprisingly the aerodynamic drag obtained is more or less same.
